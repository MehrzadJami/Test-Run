Batch Reactor: First-Order Irreversible Reaction Kinetics

This document describes a mathematical model for a batch reactor in which a single
reactant undergoes first-order irreversible degradation. This model is applicable to
chemical degradation, radioactive decay, and simple enzymatic reactions.

System Description:
A batch reactor has no inflow or outflow during operation. A reactant at initial
concentration C0 degrades at a rate proportional to its current concentration C.
The reaction is irreversible and temperature is held constant throughout the batch.

State Variables:
- C: reactant concentration (mol/L) — molar concentration of the species being degraded

Parameters:
- k: first-order rate constant = 0.12 1/h (degradation rate constant, temperature-dependent)
- C0: initial reactant concentration = 1.0 mol/L (reactor loading at t = 0)

Governing Equations:
Rate law (first-order):
  r = -k * C

Mole balance for batch reactor:
  dC/dt = -k * C

Analytical solution (exponential decay):
  C(t) = C0 * exp(-k * t)

Half-life of the reactant:
  t_half = ln(2) / k

At k = 0.12 1/h, the half-life is approximately 5.78 hours.
90% conversion is achieved at t = ln(10) / k ≈ 19.2 hours.

Assumptions:
- Perfect mixing within the batch reactor at all times
- Isothermal operation (k is constant throughout the batch)
- First-order kinetics hold over the entire concentration range
- Reaction is irreversible; product back-reaction is negligible
- Constant liquid volume (no evaporation or density change with conversion)
- Single reactant and single reaction pathway

Limitations:
- Rate constant k is highly temperature-sensitive (Arrhenius); temperature deviations
  will cause significant errors if not accounted for
- First-order assumption breaks down at high concentrations for reactions that are
  truly second-order or have Langmuir-type adsorption
- Model does not include product inhibition
- Not applicable to reversible reactions without modification
- Pressure effects on liquid-phase reactions are ignored

Typical first-order rate constants:
  Pharmaceutical degradation: 0.001 – 0.1 1/h
  Biological oxygen demand: 0.05 – 0.5 1/h
  Radioactive decay: highly variable (half-life from seconds to millennia)
